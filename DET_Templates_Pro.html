CIERRE

Template paso a paso
1
Visión General
0 – 15 s · Describe la escena principal en 1-2 frases
▼
Abre con una oración que capture el "qué" completo: quién, qué está haciendo, dónde. No empieces con "I see..." — usa verbos más ricos.
Frases de apertura (clic para copiar)
"This photograph depicts…" "This image captures…" "This photo shows…" "In this photograph, we can see…" "This vibrant scene portrays…" "At first glance, this image presents…"
2
Primer Plano — Sujeto Principal
15 – 40 s · Descripción detallada del foco principal
▼
Describe apariencia, ropa, expresión, acción, postura. Usa adjetivos específicos (no "nice" — usa "vibrant", "intricate", "weathered"). Siempre describe el COLOR de los objetos.
Conectores de foco
"The first thing that catches my eye is…" "In the foreground, I can see…" "Looking closely at the center…" "The main subject appears to be…" "Most prominently…" "Dominating the frame is…"
3
Fondo y Contexto
40 – 60 s · Entorno, setting, elementos secundarios
▼
Describe el entorno: tipo de lugar, luz, elementos del fondo. Usa preposiciones espaciales ricas y relaciones entre elementos.
Posiciones espaciales
"In the background…" "Behind the main subject…" "To the left/right of…" "Further back in the scene…" "Surrounding the central figure…" "In the upper/lower portion…" "Scattered across the frame…" "Adjacent to…"
4
Inferencias y Ambiente
60 – 65 s · Especulación + mood de la foto
▼
Este paso marca la diferencia entre B2 y C1. Pregúntate "¿Por qué?" y especula sobre el contexto, emoción o propósito de la foto.
Frases de inferencia
"It appears that…" "Judging by…, I would say…" "This must be…" "Presumably, this was taken…" "The overall atmosphere seems…" "Based on the details, it's likely that…" "This could well be…" "One might assume that…"
5
Cierre + Impresión Final
65 – 70 s · Conclusión memorable
▼
Cierra con una observación global que muestre pensamiento crítico. Evita repetir lo que ya dijiste.
Frases de cierre
"Overall, this photo beautifully captures…" "This image powerfully conveys…" "What strikes me most about this scene is…" "This photograph reminds us of…" "It's a compelling snapshot of…"

This photograph depicts a surfer skillfully riding a powerful ocean wave. Looking at the foreground, the subject — a young man wearing a sleek black wetsuit — is balanced perfectly on a white surfboard, with his arms extended for stability and his gaze fixed on the wave ahead. His posture appears tense yet controlled, suggesting years of practice. In the background, the wave towers impressively, its deep turquoise water contrasting with the bright white foam cascading around the surfer. The surrounding water further reinforces the sense of raw natural power. Judging by the wave's height and the surfer's confidence, this is likely a competitive surfing event or a well-known surf location. The overall atmosphere conveys a thrilling blend of athleticism and connection with nature. Overall, this image powerfully captures the exhilaration and skill required to master one of the world's most demanding sports.
This image captures a scientist working attentively in a modern research laboratory. The first thing that catches my eye is the researcher — a woman dressed in a crisp white lab coat and protective safety goggles — who is peering through a high-powered microscope. Her expression appears deeply focused, suggesting she is analysing a critical specimen. In the background, I can make out neatly organized shelves lined with glass beakers, test tubes, and labelled solution bottles, all of which point to a well-equipped professional environment. Surrounding her workstation are various instruments and notebooks, indicating ongoing, methodical research. Based on the sophisticated equipment and sterile setting, this must be a university or pharmaceutical laboratory. The atmosphere exudes precision and dedication. What strikes me most is how the image encapsulates the quiet intensity of scientific inquiry — a reminder of how vital this work is to human progress.
Pro Tips · Puntuación alta
⏱
Usa los 70 segundos
Bajo 30 s = score muy bajo. Si te quedas sin cosas que decir, especula sobre el origen, propósito o historia de la foto.
🎨
Siempre describe el color
"A car" → "a sleek silver car." Agregar color a cada objeto suma puntos de vocabulario rápidamente.
🔍
Pregunta "¿Por qué?"
Especular sobre la razón o contexto de la foto demuestra C1. "This must be..." / "Presumably..."
📐
Usa posición espacial
Organiza tu descripción espacialmente: foreground → background → left/right. Da estructura y coherencia.
🚫
Evita "I see..."
Reemplaza con "depicts," "portrays," "captures," "shows." Los evaluadores notan variedad léxica desde la primera oración.
🎭
Describe emociones
Siempre menciona la emoción o expresión de las personas. "She appears excited..." / "He looks exhausted yet proud..."

✓ Sí hacer

  • Hablar los 70 segundos completos
  • Describir colores específicos de cada objeto
  • Usar vocabulary variado (no repetir palabras)
  • Especular con modales: must, could, might
  • Incluir emociones / expresiones de personas
  • Organizar por foreground → background

✗ No hacer

  • Empezar con "I see a photo of..."
  • Responder menos de 30 segundos
  • Usar adjetivos vagos: nice, good, big
  • Hacer pausas largas de silencio (rellena con "Let me also mention...")
  • Describir solo el foreground e ignorar el fondo

Template A — Opinión / Argumentación
1
Tesis Clara
0 – 12 s · Posición en 1 oración directa
▼
Di tu posición en la primera oración. El evaluador necesita saber inmediatamente qué vas a argumentar. Sé específico, no general.
Frases de tesis (Opinion)
"In my view, [topic] is undeniably…" "I firmly believe that…" "It is my considered opinion that…" "I would argue that…" "Without a doubt, I support the idea that…" "When it comes to [topic], I strongly feel that…"
2
Primer Argumento
12 – 38 s · Razón + Ejemplo específico
▼
Presenta tu argumento más fuerte con una razón clara y un ejemplo concreto o personal. Sé específico — los ejemplos vagos no puntúan bien.
Introducir argumento
"First of all, one compelling reason is that…" "To begin with, the most significant factor is…" "My primary reason for this view is…"
Introducir ejemplo
"For instance…" "A clear example of this is…" "To illustrate, consider how…" "This is evident in the fact that…"
3
Segundo Argumento
38 – 62 s · Punto distinto + evidencia
▼
Agrega un segundo punto que sea claramente diferente al primero. No lo repitas — explora otro ángulo del mismo tema.
Transiciones de adición
"Furthermore…" "What's more…" "Moreover, it is worth noting that…" "In addition to this…" "Another compelling point is that…" "Building on this idea…"
4
Contrapunto y Refutación
62 – 78 s · Reconoce + rebate
▼
Reconocer la posición opuesta y rebatirla es característica C1. Fórmula: "Some argue X. However, this overlooks Y."
Reconocer posición opuesta
"While some may argue that…" "Admittedly, critics might point out…" "It could be argued that…"
Refutar
"However, this fails to account for…" "Nevertheless, the benefits far outweigh…" "Yet when we consider…, it becomes clear that…" "This perspective, however, overlooks…"
5
Conclusión
78 – 90 s · Reafirma + nota final
▼
Repite tu tesis con palabras diferentes. Añade una observación final o implicación más amplia si tienes tiempo.
Frases de conclusión
"In conclusion, I maintain that…" "To sum up, it is clear that…" "Ultimately, the evidence suggests that…" "All things considered, I believe…" "For these reasons, I would argue that…"

Respuesta Ejemplo
I firmly believe that young people should take a gap year before university. First of all, a gap year allows individuals to mature emotionally, making them far better prepared for the academic and social demands of higher education. For instance, students who spend time working or volunteering abroad often develop greater independence and self-discipline. Furthermore, a gap year provides invaluable real-world experience that complements academic study — skills like problem-solving, communication, and adaptability are difficult to acquire in a classroom setting alone. While some may argue that a gap year causes students to lose academic momentum, this perspective overlooks the fact that returning students consistently demonstrate higher motivation and clearer career goals. In conclusion, the personal and professional development offered by a gap year makes it a worthwhile investment for most young people.
One experience that stands out is a disagreement I once had with my closest friend over a shared project. To give you some context, we were co-leading a community event and had fundamentally different ideas about how to organize it. What happened was that our differing approaches led to a serious argument, which created tension between us for several days. What made this particularly difficult was that I genuinely believed I was right, so letting go of my position felt almost impossible at first. However, we eventually sat down for a calm, honest conversation where we each listened without interrupting. Looking back, I realize that the disagreement actually strengthened our friendship, because it forced us to communicate more openly and respectfully. This experience taught me that effective conflict resolution requires both empathy and a willingness to compromise.

✓ Sí hacer

  • Estado tu posición en la primera oración
  • Dar 2 argumentos con ejemplos específicos
  • Incluir un contrapunto (marca C1)
  • Usar variedad de conectores
  • Hablar mínimo 60-70 segundos

✗ No hacer

  • Empezar sin posición clara
  • Repetir el mismo argumento con otras palabras
  • Ignorar el contrapunto (pierde puntos)
  • Usar siempre "I think" — varía con "I believe," "I maintain," "I would argue"

Template Universal (adaptable a los 4 tipos)
1
Introducción — Hook
0 – 15 s · Abre con algo memorable
▼
Tienes 15 segundos para enganchar al evaluador. Usa una pregunta retórica, una declaración impactante, o entra directamente a una anécdota vivid.
Frases de apertura por tipo
"This is a topic I feel genuinely passionate about…" "Let me tell you about an experience that completely changed my perspective on…" "If I had to choose one word to describe [topic], it would be…" "There's a memory that comes to mind immediately when I think about…" "Few things have shaped my thinking more profoundly than…"
2
Anécdota / Experiencia Personal
15 – 55 s · Historia específica y concreta
▼
Cuenta una historia específica. Evita generalizaciones. Da contexto: dónde, cuándo, con quién. Los evaluadores valoran especificidad por encima de todo.
Conectores narrativos
"I vividly remember the moment when…" "It was [time/place] when…" "I was [age/in a specific situation] when…" "The whole thing started when…" "At the time, I had no idea that…" "What unfolded next was…"
3
Desarrollo con Detalles Sensoriales
55 – 120 s · El núcleo de tu respuesta
▼
65 segundos para desarrollar. Usa detalles sensoriales, emociones, diálogo. Describe lo que sentiste, viste, oíste. Los detalles hacen la diferencia entre B2 y C1.
Detalles y transiciones
"Specifically, what I noticed was…" "What stood out most was…" "To paint a clearer picture…" "What made this moment particularly [adj.] was…" "At that point, I realized that…" "As this was unfolding, I felt…" "The more I [verb], the more I [verb]…"
4
Reflexión y Perspectiva
120 – 155 s · Qué aprendiste / cómo cambió algo
▼
La reflexión es lo que diferencia una respuesta C1 de una B2. Conecta la experiencia a algo más grande: un valor, una lección, un cambio de perspectiva.
Transiciones reflexivas
"Looking back on this now, I realize…" "What this experience ultimately taught me was…" "In retrospect, I can see that…" "This fundamentally changed the way I…" "What surprised me most, in hindsight, was…" "The broader lesson here is that…"
5
Conclusión Memorable
155 – 180 s · Cierre que deja huella
▼
Cierra con una nota que sea hacia adelante — una implicación, un objetivo, o un insight que conecte con el mundo más amplio.
Frases de cierre
"Ultimately, this experience has shaped who I am today in that…" "To this day, I carry that lesson with me…" "And that, I believe, is one of the most valuable things…" "In the end, what matters most is…" "It's a reminder that…"

Experience

"I vividly recall…" "The person who came to mind immediately…" "It was a turning point because…"

Opinion

"My stance on this is clear…" "There are several reasons I believe…" "A case in point is…"
Respuesta Ejemplo (180 s)
There's a memory that comes to mind immediately when I think about a time I helped a stranger. It was a rainy Tuesday afternoon a few years ago, when I was rushing to catch a train. I vividly remember noticing an elderly woman struggling to lift a heavy suitcase up a steep staircase at the station. Without thinking, I put down my own bags and helped her carry it to the platform. What stood out most was how visibly relieved she was — she grabbed my hand with both of hers and said something I still think about: "Young people give me hope." At that point, I realized that small actions can carry an enormous emotional weight for someone who is struggling. What made this moment particularly powerful was how little effort it required from me, yet how meaningful it clearly was for her. Looking back on this now, I recognize that we often underestimate the impact of spontaneous kindness. This fundamentally changed the way I navigate busy public spaces — I am now far more attentive to people around me who might need help. Ultimately, this experience reinforced my belief that genuine connection and human compassion are among the most valuable things we can offer each other, even in passing.
Few topics generate more debate than the question of whether it's better to live in a large city or a small town. Having experienced both, I feel well-positioned to explore the key differences. On one hand, city life offers unrivalled access to professional opportunities, cultural diversity, and world-class infrastructure. For instance, during the three years I spent living in a major capital, I had access to international networking events, cutting-edge technology hubs, and a social circle that spanned over twenty nationalities — opportunities that would have been impossible in a smaller setting. On the other hand, small towns provide something cities consistently fail to deliver: a genuine sense of community and a slower, more sustainable pace of life. What tips the scales for me is the quality of human connection available in smaller communities, where neighbours know each other by name and social ties run deep. The key distinction, however, lies in what stage of life you are in — cities reward ambition and youth, while smaller towns nurture rootedness and wellbeing. In retrospect, my years in both environments taught me that neither is inherently superior. Ultimately, the ideal choice depends entirely on the kind of life you want to build — and I believe both deserve celebration for what they uniquely offer.
⏳
Habla los 3 minutos
Score más alto correlaciona directamente con hablar más. Usa los 180 s completos siempre.
🎯
Sé específico
"A few years ago" no es suficiente — di "During my second year of university..." Los detalles concretos construyen coherencia.
🔄
Usa los 30 s de prep
Anota mentalmente: gancho, anécdota, 2 detalles, reflexión, cierre. 5 puntos de apoyo para 180 segundos.
📚
Vocabulario lexical
Los evaluadores notan "lexical range" en los primeros 60 s. Usa tu vocabulario más sofisticado al principio.
😌
Pausa ≠ filler
Una pausa de 0.5 s es normal. "Um, uh, like" repetidos bajan el score. Entrena con pausas silenciosas cortas.
🔗
Conecta cada idea
Cada oración debe conectar con la anterior. "Discourse coherence" es 20% del score total.

"One reason is that…" / "This is because…"

18 – 28 s

Templates por tipo de pregunta
A
Pregunta de Opinión
"Do you prefer X or Y?" / "What do you think about…?"
▼
Empieza con tu respuesta. No digas "This is a very interesting question" — pierde 2-3 segundos valiosos.
Estructura exacta (35 s)
"I'd definitely say [X], mainly because…" "Personally, I lean toward [X]. The reason is that…" "For me, [X] is clearly preferable because…"
Razón + Ejemplo (combinar)
"One reason is that… For example, in my case…" "This is because… A clear instance of this is…"
Cierre
"So that's why I strongly prefer [X]." "In short, [X] works best for me."
B
Pregunta de Experiencia
"Tell me about a time…" / "Have you ever…?"
▼
Micro-narrativa: contexto muy breve + qué pasó + resultado/lección. No intentes dar una historia larga — 35 s es micro-story.
"Yes, I actually experienced something like this when…" "One time that comes to mind is when…" "I remember a specific situation where…"
Conector de resultado
"As a result…" "What I learned from it was…" "It taught me that…"
C
Pregunta Comparativa / Abstracta
"How does X compare to Y?" / "Why do you think…?"
▼
Para comparativas: toma partido rápido, da una diferencia clave. Para abstractas: anclate a algo concreto inmediatamente.
Comparativa
"The main difference is that… while…" "Compared to [X], [Y] tends to…" "On one hand… on the other hand…"
Pregunta abstracta
"I think the core reason is…" "From my perspective, this happens because…" "What I find most interesting about this is…"
Respuestas Ejemplo (35 s cada una)
Q: Should young people take a gap year before university?

I'd definitely say yes, and quite strongly at that. One key reason is that a gap year gives young people time to mature before facing the academic and financial pressures of university. In my own case, I took several months off to volunteer abroad, and it gave me a sense of direction that I simply didn't have at 18. So yes, I firmly believe gap years build the kind of self-awareness that makes university far more productive.
Q: Tell me about a time you learned something difficult. What motivated you?

One situation that comes to mind immediately is when I was learning to code from scratch. I had no background in programming, and those first weeks were genuinely overwhelming. What kept me going was the immediate feedback — every small piece of code that worked felt like a real victory. What I learned from it was that motivation in hard tasks often comes not from big wins, but from noticing incremental progress. That shift in mindset changed how I approach challenges entirely.
Q: How has social media changed the way people communicate compared to 20 years ago?

The difference is striking. Compared to 20 years ago, communication today is far more immediate, constant, and public. On one hand, platforms like Instagram and WhatsApp have made it easier to stay connected across long distances — something that genuinely wasn't possible before. On the other hand, depth of connection has arguably suffered; people interact more frequently but often more superficially. I think the key shift is that communication went from being intentional to being almost automatic.
Reglas críticas del Interactive Speaking
🎙
Click "Record Now" primero
Tu respuesta NO se graba hasta que hagas clic. Es lo primero que debes hacer.
⚡
Habla en los primeros 2 s
No planifiques durante 5 segundos en silencio. Tu primera idea es válida — úsala.
👂
Escucha las keyword del final
La pregunta real suele estar en la segunda mitad del enunciado. Concentra tu atención ahí.
🌊
Un ejemplo forte > tres débiles
En 35 s, un ejemplo específico y desarrollado puntúa más que tres ejemplos superficiales.
💬
Fillers naturales sí
"Well," "Honestly," "I'd say," dan tiempo de procesamiento sin penalizar. "Um, um, um" sí penaliza.
🎯
Habla ≥ 28 segundos
Respuestas bajo 15 s puntúan muy bajo. Apunta a 28-32 s siempre — el score mejora con longitud.

✓ Sí hacer

  • Responder inmediatamente (sin intro larga)
  • Usar OREO: posición → razón → ejemplo → cierre
  • Hablar 28-35 segundos
  • Usar fillers naturales: "Well," "Honestly"
  • Desarrollar UN ejemplo con detalle
  • Escuchar las keywords del final de la pregunta

✗ No hacer

  • "This is a very interesting question..." (pierde 3s)
  • Silencio planificando más de 1 segundo
  • Dar 3+ ejemplos superficiales
  • "Um, uh" repetitivos
  • Olvidar hacer clic en "Record Now"
  • Responder menos de 15 segundos
